The Problem

Google's enterprise sales team was spending hours each week crafting responses to repetitive client queries. The information existed internally — but siloed across docs, slides, and email threads. Could an AI assistant bridge that gap fast enough to actually change how a 50-person team worked?

The Approach

The system was architected as a fully autonomous Gmail agent. On every inbound email, the agent reasons over an internal knowledge base, drafts a contextual reply, sends it, and logs the interaction — all without human intervention.

Observing the knowledge retrieval problem

Google’s enterprise sales team was spending 4+ hours per week manually researching and drafting responses to repetitive client queries. The information existed internally — but was siloed across docs, slides, and email threads. The root problem wasn’t content quality; it was retrieval latency.

Scoping the pilot and defining success

A 50-person sales team was selected as the initial pilot — small enough to move fast, large enough for meaningful signal. Success was defined as measurable weekly time savings per person, not just adoption. Core hypothesis: if an AI agent could retrieve and draft in 30 seconds what took a human 20 minutes, behavior change would be self-sustaining.

Designing a fully autonomous Gmail agent

The system was designed as a fully autonomous loop: inbound email triggers the agent → Gemini reasons over an internal knowledge base → contextual draft generated → presented for human approval → sent. The critical design constraint was human-in-the-loop approval — preserving trust while removing the research burden entirely.

Building and instrumenting the agent pipeline

Built the agent pipeline using Gemini, Gmail API, and an internal sales knowledge base. A key build decision was to instrument every interaction for behavioral data capture — because this data was earmarked to feed Gemini’s internal personalization layer. Usage tracking was a first-class requirement, not an afterthought.

Piloting and scaling through word of mouth

Launched to the 50-person sales pilot with no formal announcement. Within two weeks, users were sharing the tool across the wider org without any prompting. The product grew through word-of-mouth — a signal that time savings were immediately obvious and the tool was genuinely changing how people worked day-to-day.
